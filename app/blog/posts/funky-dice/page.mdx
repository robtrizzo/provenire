# Funky Dice

For anyone following along, I have already spoken at length about [dice](/blog/picking-action-dice) on this very blog. And I'm incredibly happy with my choices. The **`Xd6`** dice pool works nearly flawlessly. In my opinion, there are a few key features it does best.

### What `Xd6` does well

**One:** success, failure, and criticals are possible at all stages of advancement. With the caveat that having **`0d`** in a stat requires **pushing yourself** and an additional source of a bonus die to be capable of critting. What's so good about this in my opinion, is that every die roll has the drama of possibility.

**Two:** successes and consequences are separate. The most likely outcome at many levels is to both succeed *and* be faced with complications. This creates compelling gameplay and keeps everyone on the edge of their seat.

**Three:** it's very easy to determine what the result of a die roll is, despite rolling lots of dice sometimes. Just find the highest die, easy! Especially valuable in-person.

### What `Xd6` could do better <span className='text-muted-foreground'>(in my game)</span>

So now that I've talked about what this system does well, I'd like to talk about some of its weaknesses. I want to stipulate that these are weaknesses *in the game I run* and are not objective flaws that apply to every game.

**One:** it's fairly constrained on advancement. The only way to create further progression is to create more skills for your players to invest xp into. In my own game, I took a fairly extreme approach to exactly that. This looks like splitting actions across two sides of the character sheet, only allowing 2 dice per skill, and color coding dice so they could be upgraded for better effect. If you're not familiar with FitD dice, that likely makes no sense to you; I must apologize but push on ahead!

**Two:** very limited mechanical texture. What I mean by this is how many dimensions of interaction a character's playbook abilities can have with the dice. We can add bonus dice, increase/decrease effect, and tinker with the cost of pushing yourself. That's about it. Not that you can't do plenty with that, but for *the game that I'm running*, I'm left wanting more.

**Three:** once players are rolling 4 to 6 dice, success is nigh-guaranteed. This is the big one. And I feel I must add another caveat. I *want* players to have awesome moments where they roll 5 dice and feel good about how powerful they are. What I've encountered is that players get to this 4 die threshold by the middle of advancement and then throw in a bonus die from an ability, a bonus die from an assist, and let's get one more from pushing yourself and, well, you have a **`>98%`** chance of success. And **`>26%`** chance that's a crit!

## How this game's dice work

If you're one of my players (*hello!* üëã), you can probably skip this part because this will be old hat to you. For anyone else curious, I'll explain how dice are currently set up in this game so that you have the full context for how future modifications will work.

Earlier in this post I referenced splitting actions across two sides of the character sheet. It looks like this:

<div className="p-6">
    <SampleActions />

What the dots represent is the color of die you roll when you use that action. <ActionDot className="bg-red-500" /> represents rolling a die with reduced effect on a success where <ActionDot className="bg-blue-500" /> has full effect. A <ActionDot /> means you have **`0d`** in that action and you roll **`2d6`** with disadvantage and reduced effect. When rolling, players pick up to two actions, one from each side of the sheet. <ActionDot /> are ignored if there are other red or blue dice in the pool.

This does a few things for us. One: it raises the earlygame number of dice players have access to. They roll **`0d`** less often. Two: it grants more room for advancement because each action has 5 discrete levels. Third: it allows narrative flexibility for the characters. For the purposes of my game so far, this has worked really well.

Other than the action scores, using an ability or getting an assist can add either a <ActionDot className="bg-red-500" /> or <ActionDot className="bg-blue-500" /> circumstantially. Pushing yourself or taking *the devil's bargain* always grants a <ActionDot className="bg-blue-500" />. And that's it. More complicated than *Blades in the Dark* but the players caught on fast and overall they enjoyed it.

## Genesys of an idea ü•Å

I am a relentless rewriter / refactorer. When I see something that gives me pause, my gut instinct is to redo it from scratch. Where this is fine in my own personal projects, this is *not* ideal for my players who sometimes wish I'd chill for a second and stop changing the game. The **`>98%`** success chance / **`>26%`** crit chance in my game was very much on my mind. I could feel the urge to refactor building! And then I thought of my poor players and took a step back. Perhaps it would be best to understand the dice deeper before I took it all apart.

When you boil FitD dice down to their basic parts, each die face can indicate up to two things: **did you succeed?** and **is there a consequence?** I thought to myself, "*What if there were only two symbols instead of 1-6?*" That would be simplifying instead of complicating... right?

When I have a problem I'm turning over in my head, I'm often talking about it to the people around me. Lucky for me, I'm surrounded by smart folks who have given me incredible advice and feedback. One friend, the very same friend referenced in [this post]("/blog/game-design-that-inspires-me") directed my attention to [Genesys RPG](https://www.fantasyflightgames.com/en/products/genesys/). When he heard that I was thinking about dice in the context of symbols and faces, he felt this would be a good resource for inspiration. And he was correct! I went off on a dice bender.

To my players: all I can say is I tried to fight my inner refactor-demon
and I lost. Sorry!

### What I liked from Genesys dice

Genesys uses a system of narrative dice. These dice are your classic TTRPG shapes, but instead of numbers on the faces, you have a collection of symbols. When you roll a pool of dice, you must consult the runes!

The fact that the dice were no longer constrained to numbers untethers them from linear math. Sure, you could fiddle with the numbers on dice, but then I think there would be a bit of an awkward disconnect. Besides, going with symbols means you can put multiple on a single die face and it makes sense! From a game design perspective, this grants full control of the probabilities. Very exciting!

Another aspect of Genesys dice I latched onto was their concept of Advantage. In their system, Advantage is meant to be a counterbalance to Threat. Think of Threat like a consequence in a FitD game and think of Advantage as an extra opportunity or stroke of luck which isn't related to the success of your current action. While I had been thinking about how to reduce my players' chance of success in large dice pools, I felt kind of bad about it. I didn't want to just... nerf them. That could feel bad. I was in pursuit of something I could give back to them in exchange. Advantage!

### What I chose not to adopt from Genesys dice

In Genesys, the player contributes dice from their character sheet to represent how good at something they are. Their dice have Success, Advantage, and Triumph faces on them (Triumph is a crit). Then the GM gives them more dice to represent how difficult the task is. These dice have Failures, Threats, and Dispairs (the counterpoints to all of the player die symbols). When rolled, Successes and Failures cancel out; so do Advantages and Threats; same with Triumph and Dispair. Once all the cancelling out is done, you see what's left and determine how the roll went.

I think that for a pool of 3-6 dice this is fine, but I see the dice pools growing over the course of play. That could get tedious and error prone. Enough that plenty of forum posts I found had GMs recommending a digital tool which will do all of that for you. So I decided that whatever I take from Genesys, cancelling out symbols would not be one.

While I was at it, I decided that the Failure symbol along with the crit symbols didn't really belong. **`Xd6`** already has a system for crits, though I'm still considering how to pare it down. And since I'm not cancelling dice out, this means that Success, Advantage and Threat can coexist in a roll. I like this.

Lastly, I decided to attempt to keep the dice as **`d6s`**. It's just a hunch, but I think the players will have an easier time adopting a new standard if at least some things stay the same. But I'm open to changing the number of faces if necessary!

#### So what's actually new?

I've demonstrated how the new symbols could be used to achieve the dice we already use, but we won't be using those dice. Instead, we'll be creating distinctions between die **`type`** and die **`level`**.

Let's get into it, starting with die **`level`**. Just as before, players can upgrade their dice rather than just accumulating more. Except instead of upgrading from <ActionDot/> to <ActionDot className="bg-red-500" /> to <ActionDot className="bg-blue-500" />, players can level their actions from **`level 0`** to **`level 4`**.

What does a die level mean? Well, it means the die gets some or more of: better odds of success, higher effects of success, higher chance of advantage, or lower odds of threats.

Let's begin with <code className="text-amber-900 dark:text-amber-200">ability dice</code>. We'll get into what the <code className="text-amber-900 dark:text-amber-200">ability</code> **`type`** means later.

#### Resistance

In FitD, players can choose to **resist** consequences, rolling some dice and paying a stress cost. In our original system, players choose what two actions they use to make their resistance roll. That's worked out quite well, but I've noticed two patterns.

1. rolling the same two actions for the **action roll** and the **resistance roll**
2. wondering how proactive actions fit into **resistance rolls**

Neither of these are too big a deal in the end, but it makes me want more simplicity. So now instead of ever needing a **resistance roll**, the stress cost of **resisting** is the number of **Threat** faces they rolled. Which brings us back to those blank faces! Where it doesn't improve your odds of success, it isn't a stress you'd have to pay in the event of a consequence you want to **resist**.

As always, **resisting** is optional. And in certain circumstances (like desperate positions), I may increase the cost by 1. But overall, this should streamline things.

#### Advantage

What is it?

**Advantage** is a resource your character can have. They can either have it or not have it, meaning they can hold onto only 1 **Advantage**. If they would gain **Advantage** while they already have it, the new **Advantage** is wasted. For those of you familiar with [Spheres of Might](http://spheresofpower.wikidot.com/spheres-of-might), this may remind you of Martial Focus.

How do I get it?

Whenever you roll a die face with **Advantage**, you gain **Advantage**. This doesn't need to be a die face you select for success; any face that comes up **Advantage** grants it. For example,

<div className="p-4 flex gap-1">
    <DieFace face="t" variant="ability" />
    <DieFace face="ce:s" variant="skill" />
    <DieFace face="ta" variant="skill" />
    <DieFace face="a" variant="bond" />
</div>

in this scenario your character succeeds with standard effect *and* gains **Advantage** because at least one face was marked with it. The fact that two faces were marked **Advantage** is just bragging points - only one matters.

What can I do with it?

**Advantage** being a binary resource creates a few ways you can use it. Primarily it will fuel your character's abilities. You'll see ability text like:

- *while you have **Advantage** you may...*
- *if you would gain **Advantage** while you already have it, you may...*
- *you may spend **Advantage** to...*

Characters may also spend **Advantage** to:

- assist a teammate
- reduce the cost of **resisting** by 1 stress
- reduce the resource cost of an ability by 1
- gain a narrative edge

## Let's talk stats

I frequently repeat the mantra *"You can't improve what you can't measure,"* so of course it's important to me that I take a data driven approach to determining if I've actually achieved my goals with this new system. So let me restate them:

1. reduce the odds of success for large dice pools
2. reduce the odds of crits for large dice pools
3. success, failure, and crits are all possible at all levels
4. roughly maintian odds of success and consequence for smaller dice pools
5. grant something exciting to players to compensate for lowering odds of success
